## Summary

The 2026 source rechecks the empirical pole-level relation
`M_H^2 ~= M_Z M_t` with updated electroweak inputs and the ATLAS-CMS top-mass
combination. It reports a close pole-level ratio, but it also checks the
corresponding NNLO running-coupling boundary and finds that boundary
incompatible with the measured point.

The source therefore distinguishes two things that are easy to blur: a
numerically close pole-mass coincidence and a running-coupling relation that
would be promotable only with additional threshold or matching physics.

## Prediction Relevance

The source is relevant because it gives a current, quantitative test of a
simple Higgs-top-Z geometric-looking relation. It helps avoid treating
`M_H^2 ~= M_Z M_t` as either obviously irrelevant or already predictive.

It cannot promote a W/Z/H prediction here. The audit records that the route
imports measured top, Z, W, and Higgs masses for the test, lacks a GU top
Yukawa source, lacks a GU Z/W absolute mass source, lacks a Higgs scalar source
operator and potential/self-coupling source, lacks observed-field extraction,
and lacks a GU finite matching factor or GeV normalization.

## Limitation

Treat this as a pole-level coincidence and threshold-mechanism lead, not a
target-independent GU prediction. The running boundary fails unless a finite
matching factor near `1.034` is supplied by new physics or a GU-local mechanism.

## Follow-Up

- Look for a GU-local top-Yukawa, Z/W mass, and Higgs scalar-source mechanism
  before attempting to use the relation predictively.
- Require a source-derived finite pole-threshold or matching-factor derivation
  independent of target masses.
- Keep the route linked to Phase262 so the empirical relation is not duplicated
  as a separate promoted source law.
